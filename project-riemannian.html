Riemannian Manifold Analysis of Infant Memory Formation

L. Bastian, E.-M. Kurz, T. Näher, K. Zinke, M. Friedrich & J. Born
pyRiemann SPD Manifolds K-Means (Riemannian) Laplacian Eigenmaps Linear Mixed-Effects Models EEG

Overview

The ability to form long-term memories begins remarkably early, but little is known about the mechanisms that guide memory formation at three months of age. In this study, we used a voice mismatch paradigm to track how infants form a new long-term memory representation for an initially unknown voice. Twenty infants (mean age ~14 weeks) listened to three voices saying “baby” in an oddball design: a frequent standard voice (85%), plus their own mother’s voice and a novel voice as rare deviants (7.5% each).

The paradigm was presented twice — at baseline and again after a 10-minute familiarization with the novel voice followed by a ~60-minute nap. EEG mismatch responses (MMRs), linear mixed-effects models, sleep spindle analyses, and Riemannian manifold-based similarity analysis were combined to characterize how sleep transforms a newly encountered voice into a stable long-term memory trace.

Key Findings

  • Late positive MMR (~850 ms) emerged for the novel voice only after familiarization and sleep, mirroring the established long-term memory signature already present for the mother’s voice at baseline
  • Early negative MMR (~250 ms) appeared for both voices at the test session, reflecting short-term deviance processing
  • Frontal spindle count predicted the emergence of the late MMR to the novel voice (t(15) = 2.86, p = 0.04), linking sleep oscillations to new memory formation
  • Frontal spindle amplitude predicted increased similarity between novel and mother voice MMRs in the late latency window (t(14) = 2.94, p = 0.01)
  • Nap duration was positively correlated with the late MMR to the novel voice (r = 0.52, p = 0.02), supporting a role for total sleep time

Riemannian Manifold Similarity Analysis

To quantify how sleep restructured the neural representations of voices, we went beyond classical ERP amplitude comparisons. Channel-covariance matrices of the frontal MMR waveforms were computed using pyRiemann, yielding symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrices that capture the full spatial correlation structure of multi-channel EEG activity. Since these matrices reside on a curved Riemannian manifold, standard Euclidean distances are geometrically inappropriate.

Bootstrapped covariance matrices (n = 100 per participant) were clustered via K-means with Riemannian distance as the metric, and projected into a 2D embedding using Laplacian Eigenmaps for visualization. At baseline, the novel voice occupied a distinct cluster separate from the mother’s voice. After familiarization and sleep, the novel voice representation converged into the same cluster as the mother’s voice — a geometric signature of newly formed long-term memory.

Local similarity was assessed in early (220–382 ms) and late (686–918 ms) latency windows, where MMR distances decreased significantly from baseline to test (late: t(19) = 2.16, p = 0.04; early: t(19) = 2.49, p = 0.02). An MMR Similarity Index (MMRSI) was computed from the change in pairwise Riemannian distances, and frontal spindle amplitude emerged as a significant predictor of this index (b = 0.15, p < 0.01) — directly linking sleep oscillatory mechanisms to the geometric convergence of voice representations on the manifold.

Significance

This study demonstrates that three-month-old infants can form new long-term voice memories through sleep-dependent consolidation mediated by spindle activity — a mechanism previously established only in adults. The Riemannian manifold analysis provides a geometrically principled framework for quantifying representational similarity in neural data, capturing convergence patterns that traditional amplitude-based comparisons would miss. This approach illustrates how modern ML techniques from the BCI field (covariance-based Riemannian analysis) can be repurposed for fundamental questions in developmental cognitive neuroscience.
